Esne – Reinventing the Milk Bottle with Sustainability in Mind

(2nd Prize in Vidrala MasterGlass 2025)

Milk bottles haven't changed much, but the way we consume and think about packaging has. Esne is a response to that shift—a bottle designed to be refilled, reused, and truly integrated into a more sustainable way of living. This project wasn't just about making an eco-friendly container; it was about rethinking the entire experience of buying and using milk.


The Design Process - From Idea to Reality

It all started with a simple question: how can a milk bottle be both sustainable and practical? That question led to a deep dive into materials, user habits, and logistics. The result? A bottle shaped by three core ideas:

  • Sustainability that works in real life – Esne isn't just recyclable; it's designed to be refilled and reused. The idea is to move away from single-use packaging and bring back a system where you keep the bottle and only pay for the milk.
  • A nod to tradition, with a modern twist – The shape takes inspiration from classic glass milk bottles, with soft, rounded lines that feel familiar. But there's more to it—the bottles stack together, making transport and storage easier while reducing space.
  • Small details, big impact – The cork stopper comes with a natural fiber cord attached to the bottle. A simple fix to a common problem—no more lost caps.



The Outcome - More Than Just a Bottle

The final design isn't just about sustainability; it's about making that sustainability easy and intuitive. Esne is:

  • Made from recycled glass, strong and timeless.
  • Designed for refill stations, cutting down on waste.
  • Stackable and efficient, reducing space during transport and at home.
  • Simple and user-friendly, proving that eco-conscious design doesn't have to be complicated.


More Than a Bottle, a Statement

Esne is a small but meaningful step toward a different way of consuming. It's a reminder that design isn't just about how something looks—it's about how it fits into everyday life. By rethinking even the simplest objects, we can start shaping a future where sustainability is second nature.
